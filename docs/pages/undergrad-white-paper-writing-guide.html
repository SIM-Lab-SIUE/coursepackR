Undergraduate White Paper Writing Guide

This guide provides best practices for writing your white paper as a team using Quarto.

Before You Start

  • Review the assignment: See Undergraduate White Paper Overview
  • Understand white paper style: Professional, journalistic writing (not academic APA)
  • Set up your environment: See Undergraduate Technical Guide
  • Plan your team roles: Who writes intro, methods, analysis, discussion?

Paper Structure

1. Title & Abstract

Title: Clear, compelling, descriptive - Example: “How Social Media Shapes Political Engagement Among Young Adults”

Abstract: 100-150 word summary - What is the issue? - What did you research? - Key findings?

# How Social Media Shapes Political Engagement Among Young Adults

## Abstract

[100-150 word summary of your research]

2. Introduction (800-1,000 words)

Hook your reader: - Start with a compelling fact or question - Explain the issue’s importance - Preview what you’ll cover - State your research questions

3. Literature Review (600-800 words)

Show what’s known: - Summarize existing research - Highlight gaps or debates - Lead to your research questions

4. Methods (400-600 words)

Explain your approach: - What you studied: Your research topic/questions - Who you surveyed: Sample size, demographics - How you collected data: Survey platform, timeframe - How you analyzed data: What statistics/visualizations you created

5. Results (400-600 words)

Present your findings: - Use clear data visualizations (tables, charts) - Highlight key numbers - Include at least two figures or tables - Explain what the data shows (in plain language, not jargon)

6. Discussion (600-800 words)

Interpret your results: - What do your findings mean? - How do they fit with prior research? - What are the limitations? - What are the practical implications? - What questions remain?

7. References

List all sources cited:

## References

[Your bibliography, alphabetically sorted]

Writing Style for White Papers

Keep It Accessible

  • Use plain language (explain jargon when necessary)
  • Avoid overly academic tone
  • Write for a general, educated audience

Use Active Voice

  • ❌ “It was found that…”
  • ✅ “Our survey found that…”

Tell a Story

  • Don’t just list findings
  • Explain what they mean
  • Help readers understand the “so what?”

Be Concise

  • Cut unnecessary words
  • Get to the point
  • Use clear subheadings

Cite Your Sources

Research shows X [@smith2023].

Team Collaboration Tips

Divide and Conquer

  • Person A: Introduction & Literature Review
  • Person B: Methods & Results
  • Person C: Discussion & References
  • All together: Proofread and polish

Consistency

  • Use same citation style throughout
  • Match heading levels and formatting
  • Ensure consistent terminology

Integration

  • Each person should read others’ sections
  • Smooth transitions between sections
  • Unified voice and message

Creating Visualizations in R

Include at least two figures or tables:

# Example: Bar plot
library(ggplot2)
ggplot(data, aes(x = category, y = value)) +
  geom_col() +
  labs(title = "Your Title", x = "X Label", y = "Y Label")

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ No citations
  • ❌ Inconsistent formatting
  • ❌ Unclear tables/figures
  • ❌ Jargon without explanation
  • ❌ Unsupported claims
  • ❌ Poor grammar/spelling

Checklist Before Submission

  • ✓ Title is clear and descriptive
  • ✓ Abstract summarizes key points (100-150 words)
  • ✓ All sections included and flow logically
  • ✓ At least 10-12 citations
  • ✓ At least 2 data visualizations (figures/tables)
  • ✓ Data analysis done in R
  • ✓ No obvious grammar or spelling errors
  • ✓ All team members’ names included
  • ✓ GitHub repository is up-to-date
  • ✓ PDF renders cleanly

Tips for Success

  1. Start with an outline – map out your sections first
  2. Draft early – don’t wait until the last minute
  3. Read aloud – catch awkward phrasing
  4. Get feedback – from your team and instructor
  5. Visualize your data – create clear, labeled figures
  6. Cite as you write – don’t leave it for the end
  7. Proofread multiple times – grammar, spelling, consistency
